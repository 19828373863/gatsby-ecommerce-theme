Tracing Cash and Net Working Capital

Current Assets are cash and other assets that are expected to be converted to cash within the year.

  • Cash and Cash equivalents: the most liquid current assets.
    • An investment normally counts as a cash equivalent when it has a short maturity period of 90 days or less.

Marketable Securities: the account where the total value of liquid investments that can be quickly converted to cash without reducing their market value is entered.

Accounts Receivable: the balance of money due to a firm for goods or services delivered or used but not yet paid for by customers.

Inventory: goods available for sale and raw materials used to produce goods available for sale.

Prepaid Liabilities: advance payments made by a company for goods and services to be received in the future.

Current Liabilities are obligations that are expected to require cashpayment within the year.

  • Accounts payable: amounts due to vendors or suppliers for goods orservices received that have not yet been paid for.

  • Short-term debt: such as bank loans or commercial paper issued to fund operations

  • Dividends payable

  • Notes payable

  • Current maturities of long-term debt

  • Interest payable on outstanding debts, including long-term obligations

  • Income taxes owed within the next year

Defining Cash in Terms of Other Elements

营运资本NWC

=流动资产-流动负债

=（总资产-非流动资产FA）-（总资产-股东权益-非流动负债)

=(股东权益Equity+非流动负债LTD)–非流动资产FA

=长期资本-长期资产

The basic balance sheet identity can be written as:
$$NWC + FA = LTD + Equity$$

Net working capital is cash plus other current assets, less current liabilities:
$$NWC = Cash + Other current assets − CL $$

Rearrange terms:

$$ Cash = LTD+Equity+CL−Current assets other than cash−FA$$

$$Cash = LTD+Equity−NWC(excluding cash) −FA$$

An increase in long-term debt and or equity leads to an increase in cash—as does a decrease in fixed assets or a decrease in the non-cash components of net working capital.

Activities that increase cash are called sources of cash

Activities that decrease cash are called uses of cash

Example: Sources and Uses

If accounts payable go up by $100, does this indicate a source or a use?

Accounts payable are what we owe our suppliers. This is a short-term debt.

If it rises by $100, we have effectively borrowed the money, which is a source of cash.

What if accounts receivable go up by $100?

  • Receivables are what our customers owe to us

  • An increase of $100 in accounts receivable means that we have loaned the money;

  • this is a use of cash

The Operating Cycle and the Cash Cycle

The Operating Cycle【经营周期】 is the length of time it takes a company to acquire inventory, sell it, and collect for it. It has two distinct components:

  • Inventory period【存货期】: the time it takes the company to acquire and sell the inventory.
  • Accounts receivable period【应收账款期】: the time it takes the company to collect on the sale.

The Cash Cycle is the number of days that it takes for a company to convert its investments in inventory and other resources into cash flows from sales.

$$Cash Cycle = Operating Cycle − Accounts Payable Period$$

Calculating the Operating and Cash Cycles

Start by gathering some balance sheet information

From the most recent income statement

  • Net sales: $11,500

  • Cost of goods sold: $8,200

Inventory period

为什么要用平均数？因为资产负债表反映的是某个时点数，而损益表反映的的期间数。

  • Average inventory = (2,000+3,000)/2 = 2,500

  • Inventory turnover = 8,200 / 2,500 = 3.28 times

  • Inventory period = 365 / 3.28 = 111.3 days

Receivables period

  • Average receivables = (1,600+2,000)/2 = 1,800

  • Receivables turnover = 11,500 / 1,800 = 6.39 times

  • Receivables period = 365 / 6.39 = 57.1 days

Operating cycle = 111.3 + 57.1 = 168.4 days

Payables Period

  • Average payables = (750+1,000)/2 = 875

  • Payables turnover【应付账款周转率】 = 8,200【注意分子是成本】 / 875 = 9.37 times

  • Payables period = 365 / 9.37 = 38.9 days

Cash Cycle = 168.4 - 38.9 = 129.5 days

We have to finance our inventory for 129.5 days.

If we want to reduce our financing needs, we need to look carefully at our receivables and inventory periods - they both seem excessive.

Some Aspects of Short-Term Financial Policy

There are two elements of the policy that a firm adopts for short-term finance:

  • The size of the firm’s investment in current assets, usually measured relative to the firm’s level of total operating revenues.

    • Flexible
    • Restrictive

  • Alternative financing policies for current assets, usually measured as the proportion of short-term debt to long-term debt.

    • Flexible
    • Restrictive

A flexible short-term finance policy would maintain a high ratio of current assets to sales.

  • Keeping large cash balances and investments in marketable securities

  • Large investments in inventory

  • Liberal credit terms

A restrictive short-term finance policy would maintain a low ratio of current assets to sales.

  • Keeping low cash balances, no investment in marketable securities

  • Making small investments in inventory

  • Allowing no credit sales (thus no accounts receivable)

Carrying Costs【持有成本】 and Shortage Costs【短缺成本】

Managing current assets can be thought of as involving a trade-off between carrying costs and shortage costs.

Carrying Costs: Costs that rise with the level of investment in current assets

  • Opportunity cost

  • Maintenance Cost

Shortage Costs: Costs that fall with the level of investment in current assets

  • Trading, or order, costs: Order costs are the costs of placing an order for more cash (brokerage costs) or more inventory (production setup costs).

  • Costs related to safety reserves: These are the costs of lost sales, lost customer goodwill, and disruption of production schedules

Alternative Financing Policies

A flexible short-term finance policy means a low proportion of short-term debt relative to long-term financing.

A restrictive short-term finance policy means a high proportion of short-term debt relative to long-term financing.

In an ideal world, short-term assets are always financed with short-term debt, and long-term assets are always financed with long-term debt.

  • In this world, net working capital is zero.

Different Strategies for Financing Current Assets

Flexible Strategy: long-term financing covers more than the total asset requirement, even at seasonal peaks.

Restrictive Strategy: long-term financing does not cover the total asset requirement, the firm must borrow short term to make up the deficit

Which is best?

There is no definitive answer.

The following considerations must be included

  • Cash reserves

  • Maturity hedging

  • Term structure

Optimal Credit Policy

The optimal credit policy minimizes the total cost of granting credit, which includes carrying costs and shortage costs.

Credit Analysis

Credit analysis is the process of deciding whether or not to extend credit to a particular customer. It involves gathering relevant information and determining creditworthiness.

The 5 Cs of Credit

  1. Character - Willingness to meet financial obligations.
  2. Capacity - Ability to meet financial obligations out of operating cash flows.
  3. Capital - Financial reserves.
  4. Collateral - Assets pledged as security.
  5. Conditions - General economic conditions related to the customer’s business.

Credit Scoring

Credit scoring is a statistical method for assessing the likelihood of default.

Collection Policy

Collection policy involves monitoring receivables and dealing with overdue payments. This includes:

  1. Delinquency letters
  2. Telephone calls
  3. Collection agency involvement
  4. Legal action

Inventory Management

Inventory management aims to find the optimal balance between carrying too much inventory and not enough. It involves understanding different types of inventory and their associated costs.

Types of Inventory

  • Raw Material - Starting point in the production process.
  • Work-in-Progress - Intermediate goods in the production process.
  • Finished Goods - Products ready to ship or sell.

Inventory Costs

  • Carrying Costs - Include storage, insurance, taxes, and opportunity costs.
  • Shortage Costs - Include restocking costs and lost sales or customers.

ABC Classification

Classify inventory based on cost, demand, and need to determine stocking levels.

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) Model

The EOQ model minimizes the total inventory cost, considering carrying costs and restocking costs.

EOQ Formula

Q^* = \sqrt{\frac{2FD}{C}} ]

Where:

  • ( Q^* ) is the economic order quantity.
  • ( F ) is the fixed order cost per order.
  • ( D ) is the annual demand.
  • ( C ) is the carrying cost per unit per year.

Example: EOQ

Consider an inventory item with a carrying cost of $1.50 per unit, a fixed order cost of $50 per order, and an annual demand of 100,000 units. Calculate the EOQ.

Extensions to EOQ

  • Safety Stocks - Inventory kept to avoid stockouts.
  • Reorder Points - Inventory level at which a new order should be placed.
  • Derived-Demand Inventories - Inventory needs derived from end product demand.
  • Materials Requirements Planning (MRP) - System to manage production schedules.
  • Just-in-Time (JIT) Inventory - Minimizes inventory by aligning supply with demand.

Conclusion

Effective working capital management is crucial for maintaining liquidity and efficiency in a firm’s operations. Understanding and optimizing credit and inventory policies are key components of this process.

Summary of Working Capital Management

Key Concepts

  • Net Working Capital (NWC): The difference between current assets and current liabilities.
  • Operating Cycle: The time required to convert raw materials to finished goods and collect payment.
  • Cash Cycle: The time it takes for a company to convert its investments into cash flow.

Cash Management

  • Involves managing inflows and outflows to optimize cash use and return.
  • Objectives include meeting payment obligations, planning for future payments, and investing excess cash.

Credit Management

  • Analyzing credit policy to determine optimal credit terms and assessing creditworthiness.
  • Balancing increased sales from granting credit against the costs and risks involved.

Inventory Management

  • Determining the optimal level of inventory to minimize costs and ensure sufficient stock to meet demand.
  • Using models like the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) to calculate reorder points and safety stocks.

Financial Policies

  • Decisions on the level of current asset investment and the mix of short-term and long-term financing.

The Importance of Working Capital Management

  • Effective management of working capital is critical for maintaining liquidity and operational efficiency.
  • Poor management can lead to cash flow issues and reduced profitability.

信用政策示例

评估提议的政策

  • 公司正在评估从仅现金政策转变为30天净额政策。
  • 单位价格为100美元，单位变动成本为40美元。
  • 目前每月销售1000个单位。
  • 在提议的政策下，公司预计每月将销售1050个单位。
  • 所需的月回报率为1.5%。

转换的NPV

  • 增量现金流入：(100 − 40) × (1050 − 1000) = 3,000美元。
  • 增量现金流入的现值：3,000 / 0.015 = 200,000美元。
  • 转换成本：100 × 1000 + 40 × (1050 − 1000) = 102,000美元。
  • 转换的NPV：200,000 − 102,000 = 98,000美元。

结论

  • 是的，公司应该转换。
